The Corruption of the Music Streaming Industry

The Problem

Music is one of the biggest forms of creative expression in the world, and it is hard for some people to balance their lives and their need to express themselves. Mainstream streaming services such as Spotify and Apple Music are the biggest forms of services for music listening, and they have monthly subscription plans to profit and keep the service running. But where does this money go? It actually doesn't go directly to the artists that make music, but it instead goes to the record labels that control the artist. For big artist, this isn't a problem unless they have contract issues with their labels. For smaller indie artists, they have to sign to a label so they can profit off of their work. The problem here is that an artist should not have to sign to a label that controls their work just so they can make money off of their work. Every year, Spotify and Apple Music raise their prices so they can earn more with the growth in streaming, but don't you think its odd how they don't increase the pay for artists in the slightest bit. It's clear that the higher ups don't care about the small artists.

The Reasons

Money controls everything in the streaming world, as we can see that with the rise in streaming over the past few years, and the increase in prices every year. The maximum amount an artist gets paid per stream is up to a third of a cent. The main reason for this is so services like Spotify and Apple Music can make as much money as simple and try and out perform their competition, it's that simple. On Spotify, you won't even be able to access your earnings for a song unless your song gets over 1,000 streams. Apple Music is increasing the pay for artists that do spatial audio, but they are also decreasing the amount that non-spatial audio artists get paid. Forcing smaller artists to adapt to spatial audio is wrong, and only exists to coropate with Apple's earbud and headphone designs.

The Solution

There are many ways to solve this problem of corruption. First we can start by not using these services all together so they will lose out on what they value the most, money. Next we can listen to music on other platforms such as YouTube and Soundcloud. These platforms have better pay and don't require a label to earn money. Finally we can call out these services for their shady selfish practices that only benefits the biggest artists and the corporate higher ups. With enough people on board, we can force these services to change their practices and implement better terms of service. We can't let coporate greed take control of basic artistic freedoms.
